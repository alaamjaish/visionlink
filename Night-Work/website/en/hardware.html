Why the mic and amp share clock lines. The I²S bus protocol has one master clock and word-select; the two peripherals just have separate data lines. The Pi is the master. All four audio wires plus power and ground fit comfortably alongside the buttons on a single breadboard.

Wiring philosophy

Two-wire buttons. Four-wire audio. One pull-up does the rest.

The button matrix

Each button is wired between its GPIO pin and ground. Pressing the button shorts the pin to ground, which the Pi's internal pull-up resistor detects as a falling edge.

A short Python program (scripts/gpio_bridge.py) listens for those edges and turns each press into a software event with debounce (20 ms LOW + 40 ms HIGH), double-press classification (700 ms window), and hold-to-record gestures.

The audio path

The I²S bus carries 16 kHz mono audio in and 24 kHz mono audio out. The mic captures S16 PCM with a constant ~2070 DC offset (subtracted per block before VAD).

The amp gets a 5× linear software gain with a 96 % soft-limiter for the 1 W speaker. Mic is muted while TTS plays — half-duplex echo suppression without an AEC.

Enclosure

Three iterations to get the form factor right.

  1. Iteration 1 — Full-cover PLA shell. Overheated the Pi in ~25 minutes under sustained AI streaming. Rejected.
  2. Iteration 2 — Ventilated enclosure. Cooler, but bulky on a hard hat. Rejected.
  3. Iteration 3 — Open-top frame (current). Exposes the Pi heatsink while protecting the mic, amp, and battery. Helmet bracket uses M3 strap-on attachment — does not penetrate the shell, preserves EN 397 conformance.
